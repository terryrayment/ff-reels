# Voice & style guide

The 50%-Canada direction in one page. Use this when writing copy for anything on the marketing site.

## The register

**Terse. Declarative. Confident.** Short sentences. Strong nouns, sparing adjectives. No mission-statement padding. The work is the argument; copy is the frame.

Think exhibition wall labels at a museum — not agency landing pages.

## Concrete rules

1. **Section labels are ALL CAPS, letter-spaced, small.** "DIRECTORS", "RECENT", "PRESS", "CONTACT". Treat them as signage, not headings.
2. **Director one-liners are ALL CAPS, declarative, ≤8 words.** Each captures a real angle that director takes — not a generic compliment. (See [99-director-one-liners.md](./99-director-one-liners.md).)
3. **Avoid throat-clearing copy.** No "We are a passionate group of…", no "Founded in…". Start at the point.
4. **One idea per sentence.** Two short sentences beat one comma-heavy long one.
5. **Lowercase body, capital impact.** Body copy is sentence case. Labels and one-liners earn the caps treatment.
6. **No taglines about taglines.** "Crafting stories that matter" energy is the enemy. If you can imagine it on a generic startup landing, cut it.

## Tone references (for direction, not transcription)

- **Canada** (canadacanada.com) — terse, all-caps personality, archive as equal nav weight, project-first
- **Park Pictures** — austere, work-forward, alphabetical confidence
- **RSA Films** — institutional restraint, geographic-first

## Anti-patterns

Don't write these:

- "We tell stories." → cut.
- "Award-winning." → either list them or drop the boast.
- "Driven by passion." → mute.
- "Our directors are…" → just list them, with their one-liner.
- "Get in touch to discuss your next project!" → "Tell us what you're making." or shorter.

## What to keep from FF's current voice

- The "Friends & Family" name itself is human and warm. Don't fight it.
- The direct-email-the-partners practice is a real differentiator — preserve it.
- "Independently run, director-led" is a true, useful frame — keep saying it, but stop dressing it up.

## Caps vs. case at a glance

| Element | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Page title (h1) | Sentence case, light weight, large |
| Section label (above h2) | ALL CAPS, letter-spaced, small, muted |
| Section heading (h2) | Sentence case, light weight |
| Director name | Title case, large |
| Director one-liner | ALL CAPS, small, on top of the name |
| Body copy | Sentence case |
| Project credit line | Sentence case, "BRAND — title" pattern |
| Year / metadata | Numerals, muted |
